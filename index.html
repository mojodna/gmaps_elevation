Gmaps Elevation

Showing elevation on Google Maps using a heatmap layer

This map shows the highest points in San Francisco as red, the lowest points with no color, and in between as green or yellow. All the coloration is relative to what's currently in view, so if you zoom in, you can see the contours of a neighborhood in as much detail as you can see the whole city.

Why is this useful?

I thought of this while trying to plan running and biking routes around San Francisco without knowing where all the hills were. By showing at a glance where the high and low points in view are, this map can help either avoids hills as much as possible, or hit the biggest ones, whichever you prefer. Or it can tell you what is the easiest way to bike out of your neighborhood. Or you can just look at the map to get a clear picture of where the hills are in your city or neighborhood if you find that kind of thing interesting.

Why not just use Google Maps Terrain View?

Google Maps Terrain View has shaded bumps to show you where the hills are, which look great but aren't really useful for any practical purpose. The one exception is at zoom level 13, where if you're patient and look really closely, you can trace the contour lines drawn on the map to see slight changes in elevation. I think the color coded heat map makes it a lot more clear what's going on elevation-wise. That said, the map does look really cool when you have the heatmap and Google Maps Terrain View turned on at the same time.

How does it work?

The map uses the heatmap feature of the Google Maps API. Heatmaps typically show red in areas where there are a lot of points clustered together and green where there are very few, but instead I've overlaid an even grid of points of the whole map, and assigned each one a weight corresponding to the elevation at that point. When these weighted points are interpreted as a heatmap, the whole map gets filled in with appropriate colors. The highest point in view is given a weight of 1 and the lowest point is given a weight of 0, so the full range of colors is always shown, even if the difference between the high point and the low point is very small. This was an essential part of the project - to be able to zoom way and examine the subtle changes in elevation in a small area.

Static Data

Data for 15,000 points covering all of San Francisco is stored in a text file on the server. When the map is zoomed or panned, the heatmap is generated using data from this file for the points that are visible in the current view window. At zoom levels 15 and above, a second file with another 15,000 points is loaded to provide increased accuracy. Even with this additional data, the heatmap at higher zoom levels is made up of many fewer points (i.e. show less detail) than at lower zoom levels.

Live Data

When you click 'Update Live Data', a series of requests are made to the Google Elevation Service API to gather elevation data for 1024 points in the current view window. Thus, the same level of detail is available at all zoom levels when using live data.
